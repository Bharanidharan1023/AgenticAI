The Taj Mahal is an ivory-white marble mausoleum in Agra, India, built between 1631 and 1648 by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan to house the tomb of his favorite wife, Mumtaz Mahal. Considered a masterpiece of Mughal architecture and a symbol of love, the complex also includes a mosque, guest house, and gardens, all surrounded by four minarets. It is renowned for its symmetrical design, intricate inlays, and its location on the right bank of the Yamuna River.
Key Aspects of the Taj Mahal
Purpose:
Built as a tomb and memorial for Mumtaz Mahal, it also serves as the final resting place for Shah Jahan himself.
Architecture:
The main component is an octagonal white marble mausoleum with a bulbous dome, set within a large Mughal garden. The complex is characterized by its symmetrical layout, with a central tomb, four detached minarets, a mosque, and a guest house.
Materials & Design:
The structure features intricate inlays using precious stones like jasper and jade, as well as contrasting tiles for the floors and walkways, creating complex geometric patterns. The white marble was sourced from Rajasthan.
Symbolism:
The Taj Mahal is a powerful symbol of eternal love and a testament to the artistic and scientific achievements of the era.
Historical Context:
Construction began around 1631 and the main mausoleum was completed by 1648, with the entire complex finished in 1653. It was built under the direction of the architect Ustad Ahmad Lahori.
Location:
It is situated on the right bank of the Yamuna River in Agra, Uttar Pradesh.
